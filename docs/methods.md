# Methods Note

## Project Background

The data presented in this explorer are drawn from the project _Mobility, Temporality, and Africa’s Future Politics_. The broader project examined mobility, belonging, institutional engagement, and future aspirations in African urban contexts marked by movement, uncertainty, and social diversity.

The public explorer provides access to selected summary statistics from a comparative survey conducted in three sub-Saharan African cities: Accra (Ghana), Nairobi (Kenya), and Johannesburg (South Africa).

Fieldwork was conducted between 1 March and 30 April 2021.

---

## Study Purpose

The survey was designed to better understand how residents of migrant-rich urban neighbourhoods navigate everyday urban life, including questions of belonging, trust, mobility, and future orientation.

The broader study was interested in how African cities are being shaped by mobility, fragmented institutional environments, and uncertain futures. The survey formed one part of a wider mixed-method research programme.

---

## Study Sites

The survey was conducted in three neighbourhoods in each of the following cities:

### Accra

- Jamestown
- Old Fadama
- Madina

### Nairobi

- Kawangware
- Kayole
- Ongata Rongai

### Johannesburg

- Berea
- Diepsloot
- Katlehong South

Neighbourhoods were selected purposively, in consultation with local research partners, because they were known to include substantial populations of domestic migrants, foreign migrants, and long-term residents.

These sites were chosen not to represent each city as a whole, but to focus on neighbourhoods where mobility, settlement, and demographic change were especially visible.

---

## Sampling Design

The study used a two-stage design combining purposive neighbourhood selection and quota-based respondent recruitment.

Across the three cities, the target sample was approximately 1,500 respondents. Each city contributed just over 500 respondents, divided broadly evenly across its three selected neighbourhoods.

Within each neighbourhood, the research design aimed to recruit approximately equal proportions of:

- foreign migrants
- domestic migrants
- long-term residents

The target within each neighbourhood was roughly one-third per group. Gender balance was also built into the design, with the aim of achieving an approximately equal split between male and female respondents.

Because of field realities, small deviations from quota targets occurred, but field teams worked to maintain balance as closely as possible.

---

## Respondent Eligibility

All respondents were adults aged 18 years or older.

Pre-qualification questions were used to recruit respondents into the study’s target categories.

For the purposes of fieldwork, the categories were defined as follows:

**Domestic migrants** were individuals who had moved from another region of the same country to the city of interview with the intention of establishing residence there.

**Long-term residents** were individuals who had lived in the neighbourhood, or nearby, for eight years or more.

**Locally born respondents** were individuals born in the neighbourhood or in close proximity to the interview location.

These distinctions were used to differentiate people with different mobility histories and settlement trajectories.

---

## Fieldwork Procedures

Data were collected through face-to-face interviews using the ResearchGO application, a Computer-Assisted Personal Interviewing (CAPI) platform.

Enumerators were assigned randomly generated GPS points within neighbourhoods and conducted interviews within a radius of up to one kilometre from those points. Within those small areas, interviewers approached households and requested to speak with the head of household or another adult with relevant household knowledge.

If a selected household declined participation, or if no interview could be completed after a second visit, another household was approached.

The use of CAPI supported:

- quota monitoring
- interview timing
- GPS capture
- daily quality control
- closer supervision of field teams

Enumerators in each city were trained specifically for this study on both the questionnaire and the mobile data-collection system.

The questionnaire was written in English, but fieldworkers were trained to explain concepts clearly and interpret key terms into local languages where needed during interviews.

Relevant local authorities were informed and permissions were obtained where necessary before fieldwork began.

---

## Survey Content

The final questionnaire included modules on:

- demographic and household characteristics
- past and present mobility
- the effects of COVID-19 on mobility
- economic activities and economic interactions
- social interactions and affiliations
- institutional and urban interactions
- political perceptions and engagement
- migration intentions and future aspirations

The Version 1 public explorer focuses on a smaller subset of variables related to belonging, perceived exclusion, trust, and future mobility intentions.

---

## Data Quality and Limitations

This dataset should be understood as a structured comparative survey of selected migrant-rich urban neighbourhoods, not as a city-wide probability sample.

Neighbourhoods were selected purposively, and respondents were recruited using quotas within those sites. This approach was chosen because reliable demographic data on migrant composition at neighbourhood level were not available in advance, and because the study aimed to ensure representation of key mobility groups.

Within neighbourhoods, field teams used randomised small-area assignment to reduce interviewer discretion and improve coverage. Even so, the data do not statistically represent the full population of each city, nor all migrants or host populations within them.

The dataset is therefore best used for analysing variation across selected neighbourhood contexts and respondent groups, rather than for making strong population-level claims about entire cities.

---

## Public Release and Anonymisation

The public-facing explorer uses a long-format summary file derived from the cleaned survey dataset.

An anonymised reduced microdata file is also provided for download. Personally identifying information has been removed from the public release. GPS coordinates and other variables that could increase re-identification risk are excluded.

The aim of this release is to support transparent secondary analysis, teaching, exploratory research, and public engagement.
